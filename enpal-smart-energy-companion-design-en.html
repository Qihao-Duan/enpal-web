Enpal Smart Energy Companion

An AI energy assistant for everyday German households that translates solar power, batteries, EVs, heat pumps, smart meters, dynamic electricity prices, and contract information into home energy scheduling recommendations users can understand, act on, and trust immediately.

Core slogan: Every kWh, routed to its best use. Country profile: Germany Goal: use · store · sell · buy

1. Strategic Summary and Reading Path

The main thread of this document is to first clarify the product differentiation, user scenario, and demo path that hackathon judges care about most, then move into calculation formulas, data trustworthiness, and engineering implementation, and finally use market research and the Octopus breakdown as evidence appendices. Readers should understand why this solution is worth building without having to read all the research first.

One-line strategy: Enpal Smart Energy Companion is not another energy dashboard. It helps households know the most valuable destination for every kilowatt-hour right now: use it, store it, sell it, or buy cheaper grid power.

User Value

The backend optimizes use / store / sell / buy in real time, while AI explains formulas, sources, assumptions, and uncertainty.

2. Product Positioning

This is not a traditional energy dashboard. It is a household energy decision layer. The product does not require users to understand engineering terminology; it directly answers three questions:

Latest slogan: Every kWh, routed to its best use.

What does this mean?

Translate concepts such as kWh, kW, grid import, and feed-in tariff into cost, driving range, and everyday scenarios.

What should I do now?

Give clear action recommendations, such as selling solar power now, charging the battery/EV from the grid during low-price periods, or prioritizing solar self-consumption during high-price periods.

Why can I trust it?

Show contracts, meter data, device states, weather forecasts, and formulas so the AI answer has an auditable basis.

3. User Pain Points and Design Responses

Pain point 1: Users do not understand kWh

Users do not care about abstract units. They care what the number means in driving range, money, or dishwasher cycles.

Energy use kWh = device power kW × usage time h Cost = energy use kWh × electricity price €/kWh

Pain point 2: Users do not know how much planning can save

The UI must directly show the money difference between naive charging, solar self-consumption, selling power and later buying low-price grid power, and storing energy in the battery for later use, instead of merely saying “cheaper.”

Charge now directly: 24 kWh × 0.35 €/kWh = 8.40 € Smart routing: solar self-use / export opportunity cost + low-price night power = 3.24 € Estimated savings: 5.16 € More advanced decision: If P_buy[t] < P_sell[t], it may be better to sell solar power and buy low-price grid power. If P_buy[t] > P_sell[t], solar self-consumption is usually more economical.

Pain point 3: Users do not know why it is trustworthy

Every recommendation needs a Why Trust This area that explains data sources, update time, and calculation logic.

AI only explains. Prices, energy usage, and savings are produced by the backend calculation engine.

7. Real-Time Calculation Engine and Per-kWh Routing Optimization

Power consumption, electricity cost, savings, and scheduling recommendations must be calculated in real time by the backend mathematical engine. AI only explains the results. Whenever device state, electricity price, weather forecast, contract rules, user deadline, or battery state changes, the backend recalculates the recommendation and writes a calculation audit.

Core upgrade: The objective is not “find cheap charging hours,” but to decide in every time interval whether solar power should be self-consumed, stored in the battery, sold back to the grid, or temporarily not used while cheaper grid power is bought.

Solar Opportunity Optimizer Formulas

Time Intervals and Inputs

t = 1...96 Δt = 0.25 h P_buy[t] = user's final grid import price EUR/kWh P_sell[t] = solar feed-in / export revenue EUR/kWh PV[t] = forecast solar generation kWh BaseLoad[t] = non-shiftable base load kWh DeviceUse[i,t] = electricity used by device i in time interval t, kWh

True Cost of Each kWh

Solar_direct_cost[t] = P_sell[t] Solar_battery_cost[t] = P_sell[t] / η_battery_roundtrip + battery_degradation_cost Grid_direct_cost[t] = P_buy[t] Grid_battery_cost[t] = P_buy[t] / η_battery_roundtrip + battery_degradation_cost

Total Cost Optimization Objective

Minimize TotalCost = Σ_t ( Grid_import[t] × P_buy[t] - Grid_export[t] × P_sell[t] + Battery_throughput[t] × battery_degradation_cost + Comfort_penalty[t] + Deadline_miss_penalty[t] )

Energy Balance

PV[t] + Grid_import[t] + Battery_discharge[t] = BaseLoad[t] + Σ_i DeviceUse[i,t] + Battery_charge[t] + Grid_export[t] + Curtailment[t]

Battery State and Efficiency

Battery_SOC[t+1] = Battery_SOC[t] + Battery_charge[t] × η_charge - Battery_discharge[t] / η_discharge Battery_SOC_min ≤ Battery_SOC[t] ≤ Battery_SOC_max Battery_charge[t] ≤ Battery_charge_power_max × Δt Battery_discharge[t] ≤ Battery_discharge_power_max × Δt

EV and User Deadline

EV_need_kwh = (target_soc - current_soc) × ev_battery_capacity_kwh / charging_efficiency Σ_t EV_charge[t] ≥ EV_need_kwh t ∈ [now, departure_deadline] EV_charge[t] ≤ wallbox_power_kw × Δt Example: (80% - 50%) × 75 kWh / 0.94 = 23.94 kWh

Device Usage Constraints

Σ_t DeviceUse[i,t] = Required_energy[i] Earliest_start[i] ≤ t ≤ Deadline[i] DeviceUse[i,t] ≤ Max_power[i] × Δt Interruptible devices: EV_charge[t] can be spread across multiple time intervals Non-interruptible devices: Once started, they must run continuously for duration_i

Single-kWh Decision Rules

If P_buy[t] > P_sell[t]: solar self-consumption is usually more economical If P_buy[t] < P_sell[t]: it may be better to sell solar power and buy low-price grid power If future_avoided_grid_cost × η_roundtrip > P_sell[t] + battery_degradation_cost: it is worth storing solar power in the battery

Real-Time Power and Energy Calculation

instant_power_kw = watts / 1000 interval_hours = interval_minutes / 60 energy_kwh = instant_power_kw × interval_hours total_device_energy_kwh = sum(power_kw[t] × interval_hours[t]) estimated_device_energy_kwh = rated_power_kw × expected_runtime_hours × usage_count

Electricity Cost Calculation

kWh = kW × h cost = kWh × €/kWh naive_cost = required_kwh × current_price smart_cost = solar_used_kwh × solar_opportunity_cost + low_price_grid_kwh × low_price + fallback_grid_kwh × fallback_price saving = naive_cost - smart_cost

Value of Solar Self-Consumption

solar_self_use_saving = import_price - export_price Example: avoided grid purchase 0.35 € foregone export revenue 0.08 € net benefit = 0.27 €/kWh

Sell vs Self-Consume Example

P_sell = 0.08 €/kWh P_buy_low = 0.04 €/kWh η_battery_roundtrip = 0.90 battery_degradation = 0.03 €/kWh Solar_direct_cost = 0.08 €/kWh Grid_direct_cost = 0.04 €/kWh Solar_battery_cost = 0.08 / 0.90 + 0.03 = 0.119 €/kWh Conclusion: Selling solar during the day and charging the EV with low-price grid power at night is more economical.

Dynamic Tariff Time-of-Use Cost

interval_cost[t] = import_kwh[t] × final_import_price[t] - export_kwh[t] × export_price[t] final_import_price[t] = market_price[t] + supplier_fee + network_fee + taxes_and_levies + VAT day_cost = sum(interval_cost[t]) + daily_base_fee

Backend Real-Time Recalculation Flow

01
Normalize signals: normalize meters, inverters, Wallbox, smart plugs, EPREL estimates, and contract prices into kW/kWh/EUR/kWh.
02
Build intervals: slice into 15-minute or 1-hour intervals and generate price, load, PV, battery, EV need, and forecast confidence.
03
Calculate baseline: calculate the now / normal cost for “no planning” as an explainable baseline.
04
Optimize schedule: find the lowest-cost plan under deadline, comfort, power limit, battery reserve, solar forecast, and dynamic electricity price constraints.
05
Write audit: save formulas, input values, sources, update time, Actual/Forecast/Estimate labels, and confidence.

Product Models, Energy Labels, and Scan Recognition

Device identification can be completed through model input, barcode, nameplate photo, EU energy label QR code, or invoice/manual scan. The public source best suited to Germany/EU is EPREL(European Product Registry for Energy Labelling), which covers product models with EU energy labels and allows consumers to search by brand, model, or registration number.

Products Suitable for Scanning / Lookup

  • EU energy label categories such as refrigerators, freezers, washing machines, dishwashers, dryers, electronic displays, lamps, smartphones, and tablets.
  • Water heaters, space heating, air conditioning, and some B2B energy-related devices can support long-term expansion.
  • Product identification fields: brand, model, EPREL registration number, GTIN/barcode, label QR URL, nameplate OCR.

Cannot Be Treated as Exact Values

  • EPREL/energy labels usually provide annual or per-cycle consumption under standard test conditions.
  • Real consumption is affected by mode, temperature, load, usage frequency, aging, standby state, and user behavior.
  • Precise measurement should prioritize smart plugs, circuit-level monitoring, device APIs, or smart-meter load disaggregation.

Contract/bill information can be read through photo capture, PDF upload, email import, or supplier OAuth/API. German Smart Meters and Compliance Reality

Dynamic tariffs require iMSys

Bundesnetzagentur explains that German electricity suppliers must offer dynamic electricity prices from 2025 onward; users who want a dynamic tariff usually need a smart metering system. The backend must clearly separate “price estimate” from “settlement-grade price.”

steuerbare Verbrauchseinrichtungen

Controllable loads such as Wallbox and heat pumps may enter §14a EnWG scenarios. The product must distinguish read-only recommendations, user-confirmed control, and automatic control, while saving authorization records.

User authorization first

Contracts, smart meters, vehicles, Wallbox, and device cloud APIs are all private data. Each connector needs consent scope, data minimization, and revocation mechanisms.

Estimates must not impersonate bills

If contract and settlement-grade meter data are missing, the interface must be labeled Estimate. Accurate or Autopilot can be shown only after contracts/bills and real consumption curves are connected.

Contract PDFs, bill PDFs, energy-label photos, and product nameplate photos can be stored in object storage. Real-time consumption data design logic: The backend should write today's interval kWh for each identifiable appliance into appliance_telemetry_readings, aggregate it by day into appliance_daily_summaries, and then generate optimizer_input_snapshots.
